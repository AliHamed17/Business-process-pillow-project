1. Executive Summary
This study applies process mining techniques to the Haifa Municipality’s job-staffing workflow (Teken — איוש משרה) with the objective of identifying delay sources, mapping process variants, and formulating concrete operational recommendations to improve recruitment cycle times.
Dataset: Two event logs totalling 1,126,436 raw events across 11,922 unique cases, covering 12 months (2024). After preprocessing, the cleaned log retained 1,040,445 events.
Key Findings:
The cycle-time distribution is heavily right-skewed: median = 1.2 days, P95 = 116.6 days.
Top bottleneck stages: CEO Decision (max 134 days), Budget Recommendation (max 121 days), Staffing Recommendation (max 106 days).
The slowest variant path (mean 317 days) involves the full external-tender track following CEO approval.
Workload volume does not explain delays: Pearson r = 0.057 (no significant correlation).
Random Forest predictive model (AUC = 0.917): unique stages visited is the #1 predictor of cancellation.
Process fitness via token-based replay: 93.92% of traces fully conform to the discovered model.
Operational Recommendations:
7-day SLA on Budget Recommendation (highest single-stage delay impact).
Automate committee scheduling (highest internal complexity stage).
Mandatory intake checklist to prevent early-stage abandonment.
14-day inactivity auto-escalation trigger.
Parallelise salary simulation with Division Head approval.
10-day SLA routing queue for CEO/Mayor decisions.

2. Introduction
2.1 Process Description
The Haifa Municipality manages employee recruitment through a rule-based workflow system. Each recruitment cycle is initiated when a department submits a Position Standard request (Teken) — either for an existing budgeted position (96.6% of cases) or a new position (3.4%). The process proceeds through five logical phases.
Phase 1 — Hierarchical Approval. The request is routed sequentially through the Department Manager, Division Head (Agaf), and Head of Administration (HR Director). The system may skip a level if no relevant manager exists.
Phase 2 — HR Control and Recruitment Strategy. The HR department reviews the staffing situation. The Recruitment Manager recommends the hiring method: Internal Tender, External Tender, or Help-Wanted Advertisement (for junior positions).
Phase 3 — Financial and Executive Oversight. Three parallel workstreams operate concurrently: (a) Budget/Treasurer approval, (b) Service Conditions salary verification, and (c) Payroll salary simulation. This phase culminates in the CEO Decision.
Phase 4 — Implementation. The Standards Department drafts the job description. The Labour Union approves the tender wording, and the Recruitment Department publishes the tender.
Phase 5 — Selection. A committee convenes to make the hiring decision. Certain time windows (e.g., application submission period) are mandated by law and cannot be compressed.
2.2 Study Objectives
This study addresses six business questions posed by the Haifa Municipality:
Where do delays occur — by stage, role, user, department, and outcome?
What are the main process variants, and which activity sequences produce the longest delays?
What is the relationship between departmental workload volume and cycle time?
Do changes in stage ownership correlate with delays?
Can internal sub-processes within individual stages be characterised and linked to delays?
What concrete operational suggestions can reduce process duration?
Data Scope: Two Excel log files covering 12 months (2024), 1,126,436 raw events, 11,922 unique cases.

5.2 Business Question 2: Main Variants and Delay Sequences
Goal: Map principal variants; identify delay-producing sequences.
Methodology: Three discovery algorithms; two-layer conformance checking.
5.2.1 Multi-Algorithm Process Discovery

The Inductive Miner (Leemans et al., 2014) achieves perfect fitness. Alpha Miner fails entirely (0%), demonstrating why robust algorithms are required for noisy administrative logs.

5.3 Business Question 3: Workload Volume vs. Cycle Time
Goal: Determine whether departmental workload correlates with cycle time.
Result: Pearson r = 0.057 (p > 0.05). No significant correlation.
Interpretation: Delays are structural, not volumetric. They concentrate at specific approval gates regardless of departmental load. “Hire more staff” is not the correct intervention — the fix must target the approval stages themselves.

5.4 Business Question 4: Responsible-Party Changes and Delays
Goal: Assess whether ownership reassignment correlates with delays.

The paradox (reassigned cases are faster) is explained by a confounding variable: case complexity. Short cases traverse more owners naturally. After controlling for event-count quartiles, the relationship is attenuated. Long-stalling cases remain with a single owner, motivating auto-escalation after 14 days of inactivity.

5.5 Business Question 5: Internal Sub-Processes within Stages
Goal: Characterise intra-stage complexity and its link to delays.
Committee stages show >150 internal events per case. The most frequently modified field is ‘Standard Time Deviation Level’ (רמת חריגה מזמן תקן) — updated when stages exceed their target duration.
Parallel-track compliance: 84.97% of cases execute Budget/Salary/Service tracks concurrently as designed. 15% execute sequentially, adding unnecessary delay. Recommendation: auto-trigger salary simulation when Budget Recommendation is initiated.

6. Conclusions and Operational Suggestions
6.1 Key Conclusions
1. Budgeting is the dominant long-tail risk factor. Max delay = 344 days. Cases never reaching budget are the primary cancellation driver.
2. Delays are structural, not volumetric. Pearson r = 0.057 between workload and cycle time.
3. The tender-to-committee corridor is the slowest segment. Mean 270–317 days — 14–17× the overall mean.
4. Extreme right-skew dominates. P50 = 1.2 days vs. P95 = 116.6 days.
5. Reassignment is a confounded signal. After controlling for complexity, stalled cases show single-owner stagnation.
6. Early abandonment drives cancellation. RF AUC = 0.917; cancelled cases never reach budget stage.
